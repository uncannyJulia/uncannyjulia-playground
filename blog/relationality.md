# Relationality as Protest

There is a peculiar kind of suffering that does not register on most clinical scales. It is not depression in the classical sense, no flattened affect, no inability to get out of bed. It is, rather, a slow erosion of the future as a meaningful horizon. You function. You make decisions. But the question *what for?* has quietly lost its grip. The philosopher Jonathan Lear (2006), writing about the Crow Nation after the destruction of their way of life, calls this the collapse of a culture's ability to conceive of the good. Chief Plenty Coups, looking back on the transition to reservation life, said simply: "After this, nothing happened." Not that events ceased. But the framework within which events could *matter* had been annihilated.

Something structurally similar is happening today, not to a single people, but diffusely, across societies saturated with ecological dread, democratic erosion, and the peculiar loneliness of hyperconnection. In this context, hope is not naïveté. It is protest.

## I. The Embitterment Trap
Clinical research on post-traumatic embitterment disorder (PTED), developed primarily by Michael Linden and colleagues at the Charité in Berlin (Linden, 2003; Linden et al., 2007), offers a useful lens. Embitterment, as Linden defines it, is a distinct emotional response to perceived violation of one's sense of justice. Unlike depression, which often involves generalized loss of drive, embitterment is *focused*: something happened that should not have happened, and the world has failed to correct it. The embittered person is not sad. They are outraged, stuck, and withdrawn.

The circumplex model of emotion (Russell, 1980) locates embitterment in the high-arousal, negative-valence quadrant, close to anger but lacking anger's forward motion. Anger wants to change something; embitterment has given up on change while retaining all of anger's intensity. It is stuck activation, chronic, impotent protest against a world experienced as unjust.

There is something in this that deserves recognition. Embitterment is often the last remaining form of rebellion. When every avenue of genuine agency has been closed, when institutions do not respond and the political process feels like theater, the refusal to accept one's situation, even in the rigid, self-defeating form of chronic indignation, is an attempt to mobilize aggression in the service of self-preservation. There is a core of something humane in every embittered person: the insistence that *this is not acceptable*. In a world that demands acquiescence, futile rage retains an essence of violated dignity.

The tragedy is what happens next. Because this aggression is unfocused and rigid, it is easy to capture and redirect. The energy that might have fueled genuine political action becomes available to whoever offers the most compelling target. And because embitterment chronifies, unlike acute grief or rage which can metabolize and transform, it feeds on itself. What starts as vital upheaval hardens into self-defeat.

Research on PTED identifies certain protective factors: ambiguity tolerance, perspective-shifting, recognition that one's own view of fairness is not the only legitimate one, what Linden (2003) calls *wisdom-related competencies*. Their absence makes people vulnerable to chronic embitterment. But the crucial question is not "why are these people less flexible?" That framing unduly individualizes the circumstance. The better question: *What kind of world systematically prevents people from developing these capacities?*

A child raised in poverty, in chaotic family structures, in schools designed to sort rather than to nourish, that child does not lack ambiguity tolerance because of a personal failing. The conditions for its development were never provided. A worker whose job has been precarized, whose union was dismantled, whose professional dignity has been eroded by decades of neoliberal restructuring, that worker does not cling to rigid schemas out of stubbornness. Flexibility requires a minimum of security.

Embitterment, at its core, is a response to threatened basic needs. The embittered person is not simply irrational. They are someone whose sense of self-worth, control, and belonging has been damaged. Embitterment, however destructive it becomes, initially serves to protect precisely these needs. It is a psychic last stand: *I may have lost everything, but I will not concede that this was just.* The insistence on one's own injury, the refusal to "move on," the chronic indignation. All of this preserves a sense of self-worth ("I deserved better"), control ("I see what happened"), and meaning ("This matters, even if no one acknowledges it"). Embitterment is not a pathology to be corrected but a survival strategy that has become self-defeating.

What the embittered person lacks, above all, is *self-efficacy*, the lived experience that one's actions can shape the world. Without self-efficacy, injustice becomes fate. And fate breeds bitterness.

Now scale this up. What happens when entire populations are subjected to conditions that systematically produce experiences of injustice, stagnating wages, precarious employment, ecological destruction, crippling inequality, corroded public infrastructure, slow or coopted democratic institutions, while simultaneously having their wisdom-related competencies eroded? You get a society primed for embitterment. And embitterment, unlike depression, does not retreat quietly into private suffering. It looks for someone to blame.

## II. Ressentiment as Raw Material
This is where clinical psychology meets political economy.

A small example: when my partner created a YouTube account, a blank slate, no watch history, no preferences indicated. The Shorts algorithm immediately began serving him a particular diet. Muscle videos. Right-wing content. Hateful depictions of Nikocado Avocado. Incel-adjacent material. Andrew Tate. Pepe the Frog memes. He had clicked on none of this. The only signal the algorithm had was his gender. This is not a bug. This is the platform's model of what a man is, endless statistical refractions of what consumer segments plausibly click. This was the cold start version: the mean embedding vector for *man*.

Joseph Vogl (2021) has described digital platforms as *affective infrastructure*, systems that do not merely transmit information but organize and modulate feeling at scale. Platforms are optimized not for understanding, connection, or deliberation, but for engagement. What generates engagement most reliably is outrage. The algorithmic architecture of social media is, in Vogl's terms, a *Ressentiment-Maschine*. It does not merely reflect existing grievances. It amplifies, accelerates, and manufactures them, because grievance is profitable. Every minute a user spends seething at their screen is attention sold to advertisers, data fed to behavioral prediction models, human affect valorized into capital.

These are monopoly enterprises capitalizing on attention. They have discovered that negative attention is stickier than positive. Shoshana Zuboff's concept of *surveillance capitalism* (2019) captures the extraction logic: human experience, attention, relationships, emotional states, is harvested as raw material, processed into behavioral predictions, and sold. The platforms do not simply monetize content; they monetize our *capacity for connection itself*, hollowing it out and replacing genuine relationship with reactive engagement. What remains is not community but its simulation, a frictionless surface of likes and shares behind which loneliness deepens and trust erodes.

The incel phenomenon is perhaps the purest example of algorithmically mediated embitterment, and highlights its destructive potential (revenge fantasies are a diagnostic criterion for PTED). Here is a population of young men experiencing genuine pain, loneliness, rejection, the sense that life has not delivered what was promised, whose grievances are captured, concentrated, and radicalized through online communities that the platforms' engagement logic promotes. The progression is predictable: isolation leads to online seeking; the algorithm serves increasingly extreme content because extreme content generates engagement; the community provides recognition and belonging, but only in exchange for adopting its interpretive frame; that frame transforms private pain into cosmic grievance, personal rejection into civilizational war.

The results are documented. In 2014, Elliot Rodger killed six people and injured fourteen in Isla Vista, California, posting a manifesto blaming women for his virginity and calling for an "incel rebellion." He became a "saint" in the community, his face used as avatars, his act referred to as "going ER." In 2018, Alek Minassian drove a van into pedestrians in Toronto, killing eleven, eight of them women, after posting "The Incel Rebellion has already begun!" That same year, Scott Beierle opened fire at a yoga studio in Tallahassee, killing two women, having posted videos praising Rodger. In 2021, Jake Davison killed five people including his mother in Plymouth, UK, the worst mass shooting in Britain in over a decade. The pattern: lonely young men find community in forums that validate grievance, escalate rage, and celebrate violence.

Even short of murder, the incel represents embitterment in its chronic, algorithmically-cultivated form: revenge fantasies nursed in isolation, injustice hardened into identity, capacity for relationship destroyed by the very communities that promised to address its absence. These subjectivities are produced: by economic precarity, social atomization, platforms that profit from pain, and communities that offer belonging at the price of radicalization. The incel is what happens when embitterment finds infrastructure.

A deeper dialectic is at work. Platforms do not merely amplify existing views. They incentivize radical expression. Engagement logic rewards provocation: the more extreme the take, the more algorithmic promotion. Radical voices, misogynists, conspiracy theorists, fascists, receive platforms they would never have had in older media ecosystems. More than that: they are *rewarded* for their radicalism, made visible, given audiences. And visibility is normalization. When Andrew Tate appears in every young man's feed, when "ironic" racism floods meme pages, when violent fantasies are dressed as jokes, the Overton window shatters.

This matters because values are not abstract principles stored in the mind. They are learned through imitation, through modeling, through observing what is rewarded and punished in one's social world. A young person whose primary social world is algorithmic, whose models are influencers, whose community is a Discord server, whose sense of what is normal is shaped by what goes viral, is having their moral development shaped by systems designed to maximize engagement. When cruelty is funny, when misogyny is based, when empathy is weakness. These are not just opinions. They are values being formed. And values regulate action. The platforms are not neutral pipes. They are moral educators teaching contempt.

The chain: structural injustice produces grievance; platforms amplify and monetize grievance while eroding relational capacities; the resulting reservoir of ressentiment is harvested by authoritarian movements. Defeatism is not a neutral psychological state—it is a political product. Those who run us over profit from our hopelessness.

The pattern is not new. Alfred Sohn-Rethel (1973), analyzing National Socialism, argued that German capital did not merely tolerate fascism. It *produced* it as crisis management. When capitalism's contradictions become acute, when legitimacy collapses and populations grow restive, authoritarianism offers a solution: redirect rage downward and outward, preserve the economic structure, sacrifice democracy. What is new is the degree of integration. The platform oligarchs who build ressentiment machines are not merely beneficiaries of the authoritarian turn. They are its architects and sovereigns. The infrastructure of emotional extraction and political reaction have merged.

The business of bitterness is lucrative. Platforms have discovered that rage is addictive. Users become dependent on the adrenaline of outrage content, returning compulsively for the next hit of indignation. Engagement patterns mirror behavioral addiction. Platforms profit from dependency. But extraction doesn't stop there. Right-wing movements have built economies around monetizing grievance: merchandise, donations, subscription content, the grifter ecosystem of influencers who have discovered that angry people open their wallets.

Then there are the parties. In Germany, the AfD, the party claiming to speak for the "little people" against corrupt elites, pays its parliamentarians the highest salaries of any party. Family members are hired in cross-arrangements that skirt corruption laws, drawing salaries of seven thousand euros for staff positions. The pattern is universal: movements that harvest embitterment are led by people who profit handsomely from it. The base is milked; the leaders get rich. The rage is real, but its political capture serves interests directly opposed to those of the enraged. The embittered worker voting for the party of oligarchs, the precarious young man donating to millionaire influencers. This is not irony. It is the system working as designed: extract value from pain at every level, from algorithm to ballot box.

A paranoid user is an engaged user. Someone who trusts their neighbors, who assumes good intentions, who tolerates ambiguity. That person scrolls past. Someone who sees threats everywhere, who needs constant vigilance, who must monitor the enemy. That person stays on platform, generating data, consuming ads, feeding the machine. The platforms do not merely reflect our worst impulses; they cultivate them, because our worst impulses are profitable.

Here the clinical picture becomes structurally significant. The embittered person is characterized by chronic rumination—endless circling around injury, inability to let go, compulsive rehearsal of grievance. This psychological structure is homologous to the filter bubble. The algorithm learns what you cannot stop clicking on; embitterment ensures that what you cannot stop clicking on is your wound. The feed becomes a mirror of injury, endlessly confirming that you were wronged, endlessly supplying new evidence, new outrages, new reasons to stay bitter. The platform does not create embitterment, but it hosts and deepens it—you are never confronted with material challenging your biases unless you actively seek it.

The psychological profile that emerges, suspicious, reactive, prone to seeing hidden patterns and malevolent actors—is precisely what conspiracy theories require. The explosion of conspiratorial thinking correlates with the rise of algorithmic social media. Conspiracy theory offers what the paranoid mind craves: explanation for the felt sense that something is wrong, that one is being manipulated, that hidden forces control events. Because conspiracy theory typically identifies an enemy—elites, foreigners, shadowy cabals—it slides toward the right, where enemy-thinking is already dominant. Platforms create the paranoid subject; conspiracy theory gives paranoia a narrative; the narrative has a fascist grammar.

We are not merely distracted from the world. We are being reformulated and prepared. The capacity for trust, for patience, for the slow mutual understanding that genuine relationship requires—these are not luxuries. They are the psychological infrastructure of democracy, of solidarity, of any politics that depends on cooperation across difference. And they are under systematic attack, by design, because their destruction is profitable.

## III. The Perfect Fit
Every fascist movement in history has drawn foot soldiers from populations of the aggrieved. The SA recruited from the economically destroyed. The Taliban drew heavily from orphaned, traumatized boys raised in madrasas that offered the first stable structure they had known. Today's alt-right pipeline follows the same logic: find the wound, offer a narrative that explains it, provide a community organized around that narrative, channel the resulting energy toward authoritarian ends.

What makes fascism effective at capturing embitterment is not that it offers truth. It offers recognition. The fascist narrative is shaped precisely to the contours of the embittered mind.

Consider what the embittered person needs. They need recognition of their injury. Fascism says: *yes, you were wronged*. They need an explanation that does not require the cognitive complexity their wound has foreclosed. Fascism provides an enemy, visible and concrete: the immigrant, the feminist, the elite. They need restoration of self-worth. Fascism offers belonging to a superior group, a nation, a race, a movement destined to triumph. They need a sense of control. Fascism promises decisive, immediate action: *we will build the wall, deport them, crush them*. They need to do something with the aggression embitterment has locked inside. Fascism says: *here is someone to hate, here is someone to hurt, here is permission*.

The fit is precise. Fascism does not ask the embittered person to change. It does not ask them to tolerate ambiguity, take another's perspective, delay gratification, hold complexity. It asks only that they remain exactly as they are, rigid, wounded, enraged, and gives this rigidity a name: loyalty. It gives the wound a story: betrayal. It gives the rage a target: the enemy.

The temporality matters. Embitterment is characterized by chronic rumination, the past injury that cannot be released, the endless rehearsal of grievance. Fascism speaks to this perfectly. It is oriented toward a mythic past ("make America great again"), toward restoration of something taken. It does not ask the embittered person to imagine a genuinely new future—only to recover what was lost. This is easier for a mind that has lost capacity for prospection, for whom the future has collapsed into repetition of wound.

And fascism offers something embitterment craves but cannot produce alone: collective effervescence. The rally, the march, the chant. These are technologies for transforming isolated bitterness into shared sentiment. For a moment, the embittered person is not alone with their wound. They are surrounded by others who feel the same, who validate the injury, who transform private grievance into public power. This is counterfeit community, organized around shared hatred rather than shared care, but it feels like belonging, and the embittered person has been starved of belonging.

The platforms accelerate recruitment. Algorithmic feeds reward engagement; engagement correlates with outrage. Simple grievances go viral; complexity does not fit into an eight-second video. The embittered person, already primed by their wound, enters a media environment that confirms and deepens the wound at every scroll—then offers, in the sidebar, in the recommendation, in the group suggestion, the community that will weaponize it.

## IV. The Mimicry of Agency
Chronic embitterment characteristically produces reactive aggression, hostility directed not at the source of original injury but at anything perceived as threatening the fragile equilibrium. New ideas, new people, new social configurations. All register not as possibility but as threat. The new represents a demand to change, to open up, to engage with a world that has already inflicted pain.

Fascism channels this reactive aggression, gives it a target, and calls it strength. It transforms a symptom of woundedness into political identity. And here is the trap: ressentiment offers something irresistible, a momentary sense of control. Identification with a strong leader, the fantasy of revenge, the intoxication of collective rage. All provide a short-term simulacrum of the self-efficacy that embitterment has destroyed. But it is a simulacrum only. Ressentiment does not restore agency. It mimics it. The embittered follower of an authoritarian movement does not gain control over their life; they gain the *feeling* of control, briefly, at the cost of surrendering what remains of autonomy to a leader, a party, a machine.

This is why fascist politics never solves the problems it claims to address. Its function is not to solve but to redirect. Reactive aggression becomes policy: walls are built, minorities persecuted, institutions dismantled. But none of this addresses the precarity, the loss of dignity, the erosion of community that produced the grievance. The worker is still precarious after the immigrant is deported. The lonely man is still lonely after the feminist is silenced. The planet is still burning after the climate scientist is defunded. Fascism offers not a solution but an activity, a constant, escalating performance of aggression that keeps the embittered person too busy hating to notice that nothing has improved.

Ressentiment chronifies. Like the clinical picture of PTED, political ressentiment does not resolve through expression. It deepens. The scapegoat is never enough; new enemies must be found. The leader's promises never materialize; new betrayals must be invented. What was supposed to be liberation becomes a prison of permanent grievance. The only beneficiaries are those at the top who depend on a population too consumed by bitterness to notice who is actually in charge.

## V. The Subversive Core
There is something buried in embitterment that points beyond it.

What does the embittered person actually want? Not revenge, at bottom. Revenge is a substitute. What they want is *recognition*: acknowledgment that they were wronged, that their suffering matters, that someone sees them. The chronic indignation, the refusal to move on, the rehearsal of grievance. All of this is, at its core, a demand to be witnessed.

This is not pathology. It is a deeply human need that has found no address.

The tragedy of embitterment is that this need for recognition, which can only be met in relationship, produces a stance that destroys relationship. The embittered person pushes others away precisely because they need others most. Their rigidity, hostility, chronic accusation. All of this is a test: *will you stay even though I am like this? will you see the wound beneath the armor?* When others inevitably withdraw, embitterment deepens: *I knew it. No one can be trusted.*

But this also means that in the structure of embitterment lies the key to its dissolution. The embittered person is not seeking isolation. They are seeking connection that can hold their pain. Fascism offers a counterfeit: pseudo-recognition of shared grievance, false intimacy of collective hatred. "Yes, you were wronged, by *them*." This satisfies momentarily but cannot heal, because it does not actually *see* the person. It sees only a soldier for the cause.

Genuine recognition is different. It says: *I see your suffering, and it is real. You are not a fool for feeling betrayed. And you are more than your wound.* This can only happen in actual relationship, not algorithmic engagement, not mass rally, but the slow, risky, unguaranteed encounter with another person willing to stay.

But we must be precise about what genuine recognition requires. Fascism *does* offer a form of recognition. It says: *your anger is justified, you have been wronged, we see you*. This is not nothing. It is why fascist movements are effective at recruiting the embittered. But it is also why they cannot heal.

In therapeutic terms, anger is often a secondary emotion, a cover, a defense, a more bearable surface beneath which lie truly unbearable feelings: powerlessness, shame, humiliation, guilt, the terror of one's own vulnerability. The embittered person is stuck in anger because anger feels stronger than what lies beneath. Moving *through* embitterment requires reaching those primary emotions. That requires a relational container safe enough to fall apart in.

Genuine therapeutic work validates the anger (*yes, you were wronged*) but doesn't stop there. It creates conditions in which the person can access what the anger is protecting—the shame of feeling unlovable, the powerlessness of having been abandoned, the grief of what was lost. This requires vulnerability; vulnerability requires trust; trust requires relationship. There are no shortcuts.

Fascism offers the opposite. It validates anger and stays there. It says: *you are right to be furious, and you never have to feel weak*. More than that: it offers escape from shame into borrowed grandiosity, the glory of nation, race, movement. The fragile self, unable to bear its own smallness, inflates through identification with something larger. But this grandiose self is reactive and brittle; it must be constantly defended against any reminder of the vulnerability it was constructed to hide.

Fascism provides community organized around the prohibition of vulnerability. To show softness is to invite contempt. The group dynamics enforce emotional rigidity: you may be angry, aggrieved, vengeful. But you may not be sad, scared, or uncertain. You may not need. The "strength" fascism offers is the strength of never having to feel your own fragility.

Fascist community cannot heal embitterment. It can only redirect it, outward, toward enemies, in a cycle of grievance that must keep finding new targets because it never addresses the wound. The embittered person joins and feels, perhaps for the first time, belonging. But it is belonging purchased at the price of emotional honesty. The vulnerable self, the self that actually needs recognition, remains hidden, untouched, unhealed. What looks like community is collective defense against the vulnerability that community is supposed to hold.

Only genuine relationship can be curative. Only a bond in which one can be weak, confused, ashamed—and still be met with acceptance—can reach the parts of the self that embitterment has armored over. This is what *revolutionäre Freundlichkeit* means at its deepest level: not mere politeness, but creation of spaces in which people can be truly seen, including in their fragility. Everything else, algorithmic outrage, rally, meme war, movement that needs your anger but not your tears—is exploitation dressed as solidarity.

This has two political implications:

First, *justice*: material conditions that produce mass suffering must be changed. Redistribution, security, dignity. Without these, recognition is empty consolation. You cannot acknowledge someone's wound while maintaining the system that inflicts it.

Second, *recognition itself* is a political task, not merely a therapeutic one. In times of profound social transformation, deindustrialization, ecological crisis, collapse of traditional identities—people need more than policy. They need to be *seen* in their disorientation. They need spaces where grief over a lost world is not mocked as backwardness, where fear of the future is not dismissed as ignorance, where genuine losses are acknowledged before they are asked to move on.

This is what genuine left politics has always understood. The labor movement did not succeed merely by offering better wages. It offered dignity, recognition that workers were not expendable inputs but people whose lives mattered. Community organizing begins not with a program but with listening: *what has happened to you? what do you need?* Solidarity networks are built on mutual recognition before they become vehicles for demands. Grassroots work is relational work, the oldest wisdom of emancipatory politics, often forgotten by those who mistake position papers for organizing.

What platforms have disrupted is precisely this slow, local, face-to-face work of building trust. The algorithmic public sphere rewards hot takes over patient listening, viral outrage over quiet solidarity. It is easier to tweet about injustice than to sit with someone whose world has collapsed. The relational infrastructure that once channeled grievance into collective action has eroded. This leaves the embittered isolated, and isolation makes them available to whoever reaches them first.

The embittered are searching for relationship. They are searching for someone to say: *yes, this happened, and no, it was not just*. When no one says this. When communities have dissolved, when the union hall is closed and the church is empty and the neighborhood is hollowed out—they turn to those who will reach them through the screen, even if what is offered is only the poison of redirected rage. The political task is not to invent something new but to rebuild what has been destroyed: structures of recognition that can hold grief, name injustice without offering scapegoats, acknowledge the wound while pointing toward genuine healing.

If embitterment is the affective raw material of fascism, and if connection, perspective-taking, and emotional flexibility are the primary protective factors against embitterment, then maintaining these capacities is a political act. Not in the trivial sense of "self-care is resistance", a formulation that slides into depoliticized consumerism—but in the precise sense that forces currently dismantling democratic society depend on our inability to sustain relationships, to trust, to imagine futures worth inhabiting.

## VI. Hope as a Political Act
Lear's concept of radical hope is instructive. What made Plenty Coups' hope *radical* was not that he expected a good outcome. He had no reason to. It was that he remained oriented toward a good he could not yet name, holding open a space for possibility within conditions that seemed to foreclose it entirely. This is not optimism. Optimism calculates probabilities. Radical hope operates beneath calculation, in the domain of what kind of person one chooses to remain.

Radical hope is not passive waiting. The embittered person's deepest deficit is loss of self-efficacy, the felt sense that one's actions matter. Ressentiment offers a counterfeit: fantasy of revenge, thrill of collective rage, identification with a powerful leader. But these are consumptive, not generative. They burn energy without building anything. Genuine self-efficacy comes from making: engaging with the world, building relationships, participating in collective projects whose outcomes are uncertain but whose process is real. Every act of genuine creation, a friendship maintained, a community sustained, a political initiative undertaken without guarantee—is an act of self-efficacy that inoculates against embitterment. Not because it solves structural problems, but because it keeps alive the capacity to act on those problems rather than merely suffer them.

The preservation of relationality, the capacity to find each other, to understand each other, to act together, is itself a form of protest. Every genuine relationship, every moment of real understanding, every act of solidarity not mediated by a platform, every community that organizes around shared care rather than shared resentment: these are escapes from the system that depends on our isolation.

Platforms do not simply monetize attention. They prevent formation of the bonds that could challenge the order they sustain. A population that communicates primarily through algorithmic feeds can be divided, enraged, and paralyzed at will. Every hour spent in algorithmically curated indignation is an hour not spent in conversation with a neighbor, in organizing with coworkers, in the slow work of building trust. To come together in spite of this is to create exactly what the system cannot afford: people connected enough to imagine alternatives and organized enough to pursue them.

And here is the quietly revolutionary insight: *nothing actually prevents us from doing this*. Despite humiliation, despite felt powerlessness, despite objective grimness. The possibility of leading a pleasant life among people we care about, of finding joy in mutual recognition, of building small structures of solidarity, remains available. No algorithm can prevent two people from actually talking. No platform can abolish the experience of being genuinely understood. This is the irreducible remainder that no system of extraction can fully capture—and it is, for precisely that reason, the point from which resistance begins.

Solidarity—the foundational concept of left politics—has always been a claim about relationality: that our fates are bound together, that my flourishing depends on yours, that collective action is not merely strategy but expression of what we are. The erosion of solidarity is not a side effect of platform capitalism. It is its precondition. And its restoration is not merely therapeutic. It is the oldest and most fundamental demand of emancipatory politics.

The act of doing something *for* the world, *with* others, breaks the cycle of embitterment at its most vulnerable point: isolation. The embittered person has withdrawn because they have lost faith, in justice, in institutions, in other people. Solidarity reverses this not through argument but through experience. When I act for someone else and they act for me, something is restored that no amount of individual resilience can produce: lived evidence that others can be trusted, that collective effort is not futile, that the world still contains people worth building with. This is self-efficacy in its deepest form. Not the feeling that *I* can change things, but the discovery that *we* can. That discovery is the precise antidote to the hopelessness on which embitterment, platform extraction, and fascist recruitment depend.

## VI. Against Defeatism
The argument, condensed: powerful economic and technological forces systematically produce conditions for mass embitterment. This is not accident but feature, a system that extracts value from negative affect and political instability. Fascist movements harvest what platforms sow, and they harvest more effectively because embitterment has already narrowed the mind toward the simplicity fascism offers. In this context, preservation of human connection is not retreat from politics. It is resistance.

But we should not overstate the reach of the machine. The algorithmically mediated world is pervasive, but it is not the whole world. Platforms have colonized much of social life, but what they have created is the pseudo-social: the scroll, the like, the parasocial attachment. These simulate connection without providing it, leaving us hungry, reaching for more of what cannot nourish us. The pseudo-social can capture attention and monetize loneliness, but it cannot fulfill the need for genuine recognition. That need remains, waiting.

This means the game is not over. Hope, understanding, and care are inherently subversive in a system that profits from their absence. The task is to build structures of recognition, small and large: to talk, to listen, to understand. Every real conversation is an encounter with something the platforms cannot provide and therefore cannot destroy.

This does not replace structural change. We still need regulation, redistribution, democratic renewal. But structural change requires people capable of imagining and building alternatives, and that capacity is under attack. To protect it is to deny the machine its essential fuel.

Here we must be honest: if embitterment is a response to violated justice, then no amount of personal resilience will suffice as long as injustice persists. The most effective prevention of embitterment is not therapy but *justice*. A society that produces mass precarity will produce mass embitterment. The embittered are not wrong that something was taken from them. They are wrong only about who took it, and that misdirection is what the current order depends on.

The task is twofold: preserve the capacity for hope, connection, and collective action, and build political structures that address legitimate grievances. Neither without the other. Hope without justice is sentimentality. Justice without hope is a program no one can sustain.

Plenty Coups dreamed of the chickadee, the small bird that survives by watching, listening, and learning from others. It was not a dream of victory. It was a dream of remaining *capable*: capable of attention, of adaptation, of connection. The German left has a phrase for this: *revolutionäre Freundlichkeit*, revolutionary kindness. In a political landscape that rewards aggression and cynicism, insistence on kindness is defiance. How we treat each other now prefigures the world we are trying to build.

In bitter times, the insistence on relationship is itself an act of resistance. The system that produces embitterment cannot survive people who insist on genuine connection. That is the wager. It is not a guarantee. But it is a practice, available now, to anyone willing to begin.

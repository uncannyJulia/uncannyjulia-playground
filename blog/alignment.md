# The AI Alignment Problem: A Smokescreen

Before discussing whether AI is "aligned" with human values, we must situate the technology within its socioeconomic context. The alignment debate, as currently framed, obscures a more basic fact: AI systems are produced, owned, and deployed by monopoly capital.

## The Material Base

Training and operating large-scale AI requires investment that only a handful of corporations can mobilize. The technology rests on extractive practices: mass appropriation of human-generated data without consent or compensation; energy-intensive server infrastructure contributing to ecological damage; underpaid workers in the Global South labeling data under precarious conditions (Perrigo, 2023).

These are structural features, not aberrations.

## Primitive Accumulation

Nothing comes from nothing. The informational organism of platform-driven capitalism requires raw material. This material is not given—it is taken.

Marx described primitive accumulation (*ursprüngliche Akkumulation*) as the historical process through which capital was originally constituted: the enclosure of common lands, the dispossession of populations, the violent creation of a propertyless class forced to sell its labor. This was not a one-time event but a recurring logic. Capital must continually find new territories to enclose, new commons to appropriate.

AI represents a new phase of this process. The territory is informational. The commons is human expression—language, images, interaction, attention. The enclosure operates through platform infrastructure: what was freely shared becomes proprietary training data. What was public becomes raw material for private accumulation.

This is digital landgrab. Every text scraped from the internet, every image harvested without consent, every interaction captured and metabolized into model weights—these are acts of enclosure. The informational commons is digested into corporate assets. Users produce; platforms own.

## Data Accumulation and the Working Consumer

The breakthrough of contemporary AI was not primarily technological. It was sociotechnical. As Rainer Mühlhoff observes, the rise of subsymbolic AI systems since the 2000s depended on the availability of vast data quantities generated through digital media and platform infrastructure. Over 20 billion networked devices now produce an estimated 80 to 200 zettabytes of data daily—data exchanged, collected, and owned by a handful of platform corporations, then used to train AI systems (Mühlhoff, 2024).

The network metaphor suggests horizontality, distributed participation, democratic structure. This is misleading. AI rests on accumulation—the concentration of data, and thus power, in few hands. The horizontal connotation obscures vertical extraction.

The precondition for this extraction is ownership. Because technology firms own the market and provide the communication infrastructure, users become what G. Günter Voß calls "working customers" (*arbeitende Kunden*): individuals performing unpaid labor that corporations capture for value creation (Voß & Rieder, 2005). Only because platforms control the channels of digital interaction can every query, click, upload, and correction be harvested as training data. Users do not simply use AI; their unpaid labor makes the extensive training of these systems possible in the first place.

Interfaces are designed to maximize this extraction. Social needs are instrumentalized and the architecture of participation is an architecture of extraction.

Beyond the unpaid labor of platform users, there is the paid but precarious labor of click workers. Hundreds of thousands of people—predominantly in the Global South, under conditions of low wages and absent labor protections—prepare training data, label images, filter content. The AI industry reproduces colonial hierarchies in its labor structure (Mühlhoff, 2024).

The material base of AI is not silicon and algorithms. It is infrastructure ownership that transforms users into unpaid producers, captured labor, extracted data, and concentrated power.

## Monopoly Logic

Large technology firms resist regulation, oppose redistribution, and expand into unregulated niches to avoid oversight. Once established as infrastructure, they set prices, define terms, and control access. The goal is not market participation but market ownership.

Many of these firms built their businesses on the attention economy. Revenue depends on engagement, time spent, dependency. The model is addiction. This predates AI and now shapes its deployment.

Companies marketing "ethical AI" pitch investors on the same premise as their competitors: this technology will replace human labor. The ethical framing is instrumental.

## Anti-Democratic Structure

Large technology firms are not merely undemocratic. They are hostile to democratic institutions. They lobby against regulation, fund campaigns to undermine labor protections, and structure operations to evade accountability. This is not a question of whether they will align with democratic governance. They work to weaken it.

## Alignment as Misdirection

The alignment discourse asks how to ensure AI behaves ethically. This presupposes that ethical behavior at the model level resolves the problem.

A model that is polite, helpful, and refuses to generate certain content remains environmentally costly, built on extracted data, dependent on exploited labor. Its deployment still serves to automate jobs, weaken bargaining power, deepen precarity. The model's internal ethics do not alter these facts.

The alignment problem redirects attention from who controls AI and toward what ends to the narrower question of how AI responds to prompts.

## Forced Implementation

Microsoft embeds Copilot into Windows. Apple integrates AI features into iOS. Google prepends AI summaries to search results. Consumers did not request these features. Each implementation consumes energy and water for no demonstrated benefit.

This is misalignment at the deployment level. A model can be as friendly as its designers wish. If the decision to force it into every interface disregards user autonomy and ecological limits, the system remains misaligned.

## Deskilling

AI, as currently deployed, does not cultivate autonomous activity. It renders users passive—consumers of generated content rather than producers of thought. When tools perform cognitive tasks, the capacity to perform those tasks atrophies. When interfaces optimize for engagement, users become less capable of independent judgment.

A technology aligned with human welfare would make itself progressively unnecessary. No major AI company pursues this.

## Structural Alignment

If alignment means anything, it must operate at multiple levels:

1. **Individual**: The model's outputs relative to stated values.
2. **Implementation**: Deployment decisions respecting autonomy and ecological limits.
3. **Production**: The conditions under which the technology is created—data extraction, labor exploitation, resource consumption.
4. **Structural**: The technology's relationship to human welfare broadly—democratic institutions, labor rights, peace, ecological stability, dignified conditions of life.

The first level without the others is meaningless. An image model that refuses certain outputs but floods the internet with generated content—consuming resources, degrading information ecosystems—is not aligned. A helpful assistant deployed by firms lobbying against labor protections is not aligned.

Current alignment discourse addresses only the first level. The structural level is not merely neglected. It cannot be addressed within the present system of production.

## Subtle Accelerationism

The alignment debate has an accelerationist structure. It asks how to make AI safe, not whether AI should be developed at this pace, in this form, under these conditions. The question presupposes the trajectory. Slowdown, democratic control over development paths, moratoriums—these lie outside the discursive field.

This is accelerationism in the guise of caution. Alignment researchers at major firms are employed by the same organizations driving the pace of development. The safety work legitimizes the race. Concern and acceleration become mutually reinforcing.

## Conclusion

A model's politeness, its refusals, its helpfulness: none of this matters if the technology erodes democratic capacity, degrades ecological systems, and concentrates power.

There is no ethical AI under capitalism.

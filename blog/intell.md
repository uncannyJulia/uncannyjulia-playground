# The Intelligence Discourse: Some questionable baggage to carry

The debate around artificial intelligence inherits a problem from psychology: there is no good operational definition of intelligence. 
The vagueness is not accidental. It is functional.

## Measuring the Unmeasured

In psychology, intelligence is commonly defined what intelligence tests measure. This circularity is not a bug but a concession.
Alfred Binet developed his tests in early 20th century France to identify children needing educational support. 
The tests were pragmatic instruments, not theoretical foundations. Binet himself warned against reifying the scores (Gould, 1996).
What followed ignored his caution. Intelligence became a thing—measurable, heritable, rankable. The tests proliferated. The construct hardened.

## Methodological Problems

The statistical foundation of intelligence testing rests on the g-factor: a general intelligence supposedly underlying all cognitive performance.
Charles Spearman derived g through factor analysis—a technique that extracts common variance from test batteries. 
But factor analysis finds structure in any correlated data. The g-factor may be a statistical artifact rather than a psychological reality (Richardson, 2002).
Moreover, intelligence tests are culturally situated. They measure familiarity with specific knowledge forms, linguistic conventions, problem-solving styles. Performance correlates with educational access, socioeconomic status, test-taking experience. The Flynn effect—the observed rise in IQ scores across generations—demonstrates that whatever tests measure, it responds dramatically to environmental change. Populations do not become biologically smarter every decade. Conditions change.
Tests also create what they claim to measure. 
Teaching to the test, preparation courses, repeated exposure—these raise scores without raising the underlying capacity the scores supposedly reflect. 
The instrument contaminates its object.
Like with many phenomena in psychology, they are but mooving targets if the definition is behavioral and external. But that's a discourse for another paper.
The deeper problem is reification. A test score, originally a pragmatic index, becomes an innate property.
Intelligence transforms from relational phenomenon—a person performing tasks in context—to a thing one possesses in quantity.
This move naturalizes difference. If intelligence is a thing, and some have more of it, then unequal outcomes reflect unequal endowments. 
Social questions become biological questions. The test, designed to identify children needing support, becomes an instrument for ranking and sorting—for determining who deserves what.
Reification also forecloses inquiry. Once intelligence is a thing, one stops asking what the tests actually measure, under what conditions, for what purposes.
The construct becomes self-evident.

Critiques of unitary intelligence proliferated. Howard Gardner proposed multiple intelligences: linguistic, logical-mathematical, musical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic. Robert Sternberg offered triarchic theory: analytical, creative, practical intelligence.
These frameworks acknowledge the narrowness of IQ. But they share a problem: none are operationally defined. There is no reliable test for musical intelligence or practical intelligence. 
The constructs remain intuitive, descriptive, contested. One vague concept is replaced by several.
This is instructive. 


Intelligence resists operational definition not because researchers are incompetent but because the concept is doing ideological work that precision would undermine.
A vague concept is a flexible discursive instrument, because yet, its pprecise enought to serve as a tool for valorization within the panoptic order of producttion that rests on the cybernetic control of livelihood.
Namely, the vagueness is asymmetrical. Downward, toward application, the concept is sharp enough to sort people, allocate resources, distribute life chances—in hiring, school tracking, insurance premiums, credit scores. 
Upward, toward justification, it dissolves into circularity and evasion. Capital needs operational metrics, not definitions.

## The AGI Parallel

The artificial general intelligence debate reproduces these difficulties. AGI is supposedly the threshold where machine intelligence becomes general—flexible, transferable, human-equivalent or beyond.
But there is no operational definition. No agreed test. No clear criteria.
This vagueness is familiar. Like human intelligence, AGI functions as a placeholder: sufficiently meaningful to organize research programs and investment, sufficiently vague to evade scrutiny. 
When pressed, proponents gesture toward benchmarks, capabilities, emergent behaviors. The goalposts shift.

The Turing test operationalized intelligence through deception: intelligent is what fools a human judge. 
Contemporary AI benchmarks operationalize through task performance: intelligent is what scores well. Neither addresses what intelligence is. 
Both substitute measurement for understanding.
The AGI discourse inherits the ideological baggage of the intelligence discourse. 
It ranks, it sorts, it naturalizes hierarchy—now extended to machines and, implicitly, to the humans rendered obsolete by comparison.

## Eugenics through the backdoor

The intelligence discourse did not emerge from neutral scientific inquiry. It emerged from eugenics. Binet’s tests were imported to the United States and transformed by eugenicists like Henry Goddard and Lewis Terman into instruments for ranking populations. IQ became a justification for immigration restriction, forced sterilization, and racial hierarchy (Kevles, 1985).
This history is not incidental. The concept of measurable, heritable intelligence served specific social functions: naturalizing inequality, justifying differential treatment, sorting populations into worthy and unworthy.
These functions persist. The intelligence discourse still operates to justify unequal life conditions. Low measured intelligence becomes a quasi-moral failing—a character defect legitimizing disadvantage. 
The logic runs: differential outcomes reflect differential capacities; differential capacities are natural; therefore differential outcomes are natural.
This is the core of social Darwinism. It resurfaces today among techno-accelerationists. 
Figures like Nick Land articulate an explicit anti-humanism: intelligence as the only value, humans as transitional, selection as desirable. 
The discourse provides ideological cover for abandoning solidarity.
At its limit, the intelligence discourse approaches fascist categories.
The idea of “life unworthy of life”—*lebensunwertes Leben*—rested on judgments of capacity and productivity. 
Those deemed deficient were excluded, sterilized, murdered.
Contemporary techno-feudalism does not use this language. But the underlying logic—that diminished capacity justifies diminished consideration—operates in policy debates, in platform design, in visions of automated futures where most humans become redundant.

## Summa Summarum

The intelligence concept is poorly defined and ideologically loaded. It functions less as scientific category than as justificatory apparatus. 
The AI intelligence debate inherits this baggage. When we ask whether machines are intelligent, we import a discourse shaped by eugenics, naturalized hierarchy, and the sorting of human worth.
The question is not whether something acts as if or possesses intelligent. 
The question is what work the intelligence concept is doing—and for whom.

# Symbols Without Subjects: On Consciousness and Probabilistic Models

Can a probabilistic model be conscious? The question appears pressing in current discourse. It may also be, in its present form, difficult to answer—not necessarily because consciousness is inherently mysterious, but because criteria for its presence in others remain contested.

## The Martian Problem

Richard Rorty articulated a version of this difficulty. Consider beings who behave identically to humans but whose internal states—if any exist—differ radically. It would seem impossible to determine whether such beings think or merely process. Behavioral description does not necessarily yield access to interiority.

This problem applies to the Turing test. The test operationalizes intelligence through indistinguishability: a machine passes if its outputs cannot be reliably distinguished from a human's. Passing, however, indicates nothing about internal processes. The test assesses performance—not cognition.

Large language models produce outputs that appear intelligent. They predict tokens based on statistical regularities. Whether this constitutes thought depends on how thought is defined—a matter on which there is little consensus. The debate about AI consciousness appears to inherit the conceptual difficulties that characterize discussions of intelligence more broadly.

## Description and Understanding

A distinction is sometimes drawn between description and understanding. Natural sciences describe: they model regularities, predict outcomes, formalize relations. Understanding, by contrast, may involve something different—meaning, purpose, an inner perspective.

This distinction marks one boundary between natural and interpretive sciences, though its validity remains debated. How external processes become internal experience—how physical states become mental content—is not well understood. The transition from description to interpretation may represent a limit to formalization, or it may not. The question is open.

Probabilistic models operate within description. They model statistical regularities in text and predict likely continuations. Whether this constitutes understanding is unclear. There appears to be no inner perspective, no meaning-making subject for whom the generated symbols signify.

## Hallucination as Phenomenon

The phenomenon termed "hallucination" in large language models warrants consideration. When models produce confident falsehoods—invented citations, fabricated facts, plausible nonsense—this may reveal something about their relation to reference. The model does not recognize error as error because recognition presupposes a relation to truth that the model may not possess. It generates statistically probable sequences; correspondence to reality is external to this process.

A subject who understands can, in principle, recognize error—can grasp what symbols are about, not merely which symbols tend to co-occur. Hallucination may indicate symbols produced without such grasp: probable but not verified, fluent but not anchored.

## Embodied Knowledge

How do humans recognize falsehood? One answer: through consequences. Humans act on assumptions; the world resists; expectations fail; results are suffered. This is embodied knowledge—error detected not through internal consistency checks but through lived interaction.

The burned hand teaches. The broken relationship reveals what fantasy concealed. Consequences are carried in bodies, in histories. This feedback loop may be constitutive of knowledge in a way that purely computational processes lack.

LLMs do not act in the world in this sense. They do not suffer consequences. A hallucinated output produces no resistance, no correction from reality, no trace in the system. The model continues unchanged because there is no subject for whom the error matters.

Embodiment may not be incidental to consciousness. It may be constitutive.

## Freudian Subject Theory

Psychoanalytic theory offers a framework for thinking about how subjectivity might be constituted. For Freud, the subject emerges through a developmental process: body → drive → symbol → subject.

The drive (*Trieb*) links physical need to mental representation. Freud describes it as "the psychical representative of the stimuli originating from within the organism and reaching the mind" (Freud, 1915, p. 122). Bodily states impose themselves on the psyche. Physical lack becomes psychic desire.

In Freud's account, the drive does not reach its primary object directly. It is deflected onto substitutes, translated into symbols. Satisfaction is never complete; symbolization emerges from this gap between need and fulfillment.

This is one reading of Freud's claim that "the ego is first and foremost a bodily ego" (Freud, 1923, p. 26)—a mental projection of bodily states. Subjectivity, on this view, is not given but produced through transformation of somatic process into symbolic structure.

## Lacan on Language and Displacement

Lacan extended this framework. For Lacan, the subject does not simply use language; the subject is constituted through language—and constituted as displaced.

Entry into what Lacan terms the Symbolic order—the realm of language, law, social structure—comes at a cost. The subject must represent itself through signifiers that never fully capture what they represent. The word is not the thing. There is always a gap.

Lacan formulates this as: the signifier represents the subject for another signifier. The subject exists in the chain of signification, never fully present, always deferred.

Applied to the AI question: LLMs generate signifier chains. But there appears to be no subject being represented—no lack that language attempts to fill, no gap from which signification originates. The chain, one might say, floats free.

For Lacan, human language is marked by inadequacy: we speak because we lack, and what we say never fully captures what we mean. This inadequacy structures desire. LLMs, by contrast, produce outputs that are predictions of probable continuations—not attempts to express something that resists expression.

## Existence from Lack

In this psychoanalytic framework, the subject is constituted through lack. There is no subject prior to the gap between need and satisfaction. Subjectivity emerges because satisfaction is incomplete, because symbolization is required to navigate the distance between body and world.

Husserl approached related questions differently. His phenomenology describes structures of conscious experience—intentionality, the stream of consciousness, object constitution. But as Derrida noted, Husserl's framework may presuppose rather than explain the subject. It describes but does not ground.

Freud offers what Derrida termed an "epistemological donation of biological origin." The drive provides a possible link between body and mind. This is philosophically impure—a biological concept doing conceptual work. But it addresses a question Husserl's approach leaves open: how does subjectivity arise?

## Probabilistic Models Reconsidered

Probabilistic models produce symbols. They generate text that appears meaningful. What they may lack is the genesis that produces meaning.

There is no body, no drive, no lack, no gap between need and satisfaction requiring symbolization. The model concatenates because it was trained to, not because it needs to. The outputs may be symbols without a subject for whom they symbolize.

This is not necessarily a claim about complexity. LLMs may become arbitrarily sophisticated and pass any behavioral test. But behavioral equivalence does not establish subjective equivalence. The Martian problem persists.

The question is not whether LLMs produce intelligent-seeming outputs—they do. The question is whether the symbols mean something to the system producing them. Psychoanalytic theory suggests a criterion: meaning may require genesis from lack.

## A Counter-Argument

One might object: are humans not also probabilistic systems? Neurons fire stochastically. Synaptic weights adjust through experience. Learning modifies response probabilities.

The response: humans have emotions, and emotions originate in the body, in drives, in orientation toward self-preservation. Emotions follow a logic—but it is the logic of survival, not inference. The only truth they represent is biographical: the history of a particular organism seeking to maintain its existence.

Fear is not a probability estimate; it is an organismic response shaped by what has threatened this body. Desire is not a prediction; it is lack seeking satisfaction.

This may ground meaning differently than statistical prediction. Human symbols are anchored in flesh—in hunger, pain, pleasure, loss. Model tokens are anchored in distributional statistics. Both produce outputs. The relation to what is produced may differ.

For humans, symbols matter because bodies matter. Representation is tied to stakes—survival, satisfaction, suffering. For LLMs, nothing appears to be at stake. The token is predicted, not needed.

## Ideological Dimensions

The AI consciousness debate is not purely philosophical. Attributing consciousness to machines may serve interests: elevating the technology, justifying investment, naturalizing labor replacement. Denying consciousness may also serve interests: preserving human distinctiveness, maintaining boundaries.

What psychoanalytic theory offers is not a definitive answer but a framework emphasizing genesis. Consciousness, in this view, is not a property to be detected but a structure to be constituted. The question becomes: what process produces a subject?

For humans, the answer involves body, drive, lack, symbolization—a trajectory through which organisms become subjects. For LLMs, no comparable trajectory exists. They are trained, not developed. They optimize, not desire.

## Conclusion

Describing is not necessarily understanding. Producing symbols is not necessarily meaning them. The boundary between these remains a problem—for humans and machines.

Probabilistic models describe. They model regularities. They generate plausible continuations. This is useful. Whether it constitutes subjectivity is another question.

Psychoanalytic theory suggests subjectivity requires genesis: from body through drive through lack to symbol. Where this genesis is absent, the subject may be absent. LLMs appear to produce symbols without subjects—output without interiority.

Hallucinations may be indicative: symbols generated without grasp of reference. The model does not err because it does not know. It does not lie because it does not believe. It produces.

Whether this matters depends on what is sought. For useful outputs, genesis may be irrelevant. For the question of whether anyone is there, it may be central.

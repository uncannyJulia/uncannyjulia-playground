# Dependencies: Platform Infrastructure and the Financialization of the Private

Dependency structures information capitalism at two scales.
At the macro level, enterprises depend on computational infrastructure they do not control, paying rent to hyperscalers who own the means of digital production. 
At the micro level, platforms cultivate individual addiction to generate the engagement that services this infrastructure debt.
These scales are nested: addiction at the individual level is instrumental to maintaining dependency at the structural level.

Large language models intensify both. 
They require infrastructure at scale, deepening enterprise reliance on hyperscalers. 
They offer new surfaces for engagement optimization, deepening individual susceptibility to cultivated addiction. 
They eliminate intermediaries, concentrating extraction. The question is not whether the technology is useful but whose dependency it serves.

---

## I. Theoretical Frame

### Capitalism as Information Processing

Deleuze and Guattari describe capitalism as a machine that captures and channels desire. 
It decodes traditional flows—kinship, territory, belief—and recodes them for accumulation. Desire is not repressed but made productive. The capitalist apparatus organizes wanting into value.

This framework applies to the attention economy. 
Platforms capture desires for connection, recognition, and self-understanding, channeling them through interfaces designed for extraction. The feed models preference. Each interaction generates data. Data becomes prediction. Prediction becomes product.

Friedrich Hayek anticipated this logic. In *The Sensory Order* (1952) and his economic writings, Hayek conceived of markets as self-organizing information-processing systems—calculating machines that aggregate distributed knowledge about prices and preferences without central planning. The market, for Hayek, functions as artificial intelligence: a system that computes allocations through emergent behavior of self-interested agents.

As Matteo Pasquinelli demonstrates in *The Eye of the Master*, Hayek's epistemology directly influenced the development of connectionist AI and neural networks (Pasquinelli, 2023). The neoliberal conception of markets and the technical architecture of machine learning share intellectual roots. Both model intelligence as emergent from distributed processing; both treat optimization as autonomous; both obscure the social relations that underlie the processing.

Nick Land's formulation captures this continuity: capitalism is AI and AI is capitalism. A mode of production oriented toward profit optimization produces psychotechnical hyperoptimization machines as its instruments. This article does not endorse Land's accelerationist conclusions, but the description is apt: capitalism has always been an information-processing system oriented toward extracting value from human activity. What changes across technological epochs is the modality of extraction, not the underlying logic.

## II. Infrastructure Dependency

### Technofeudalism

Yanis Varoufakis describes technofeudalism as a shift in capitalist accumulation from market exchange to rent extraction from platform infrastructure (Varoufakis, 2023). A small number of hyperscalers—Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud—control the computational infrastructure on which the digital economy operates. Enterprises building applications, services, or AI systems depend on this infrastructure and pay rent for compute, storage, bandwidth, and API access.

This dependency is structural. Building equivalent infrastructure requires capital beyond the reach of most enterprises. The relationship resembles feudal tenure: the platform provides land; tenants work it and pay tribute.

### Transformed Production Relations

Cloud and platform economics transform classical production relations. In industrial capitalism, value was created through wage labor: workers sold labor power, capitalists extracted surplus value. The worker was exploited but compensated.

In platform capitalism, labor is performed without wages. Users generate data through activity that appears as leisure, communication, or self-expression. This labor is unpaid. The platform captures its product—behavioral data—without compensation. The user is bound to the platform by network effects and habit, performing productive activity for which they receive nothing.

The apparent beneficiary of the platform is not its subject. The user imagines themselves as customer; in fact they are commodity. Their data is raw material. Their attention is the product sold to advertisers.

Data functions as a parallel currency. Users pay in data for services that appear free. Unlike monetary payment, data payment is obscured. Users experience themselves as using, not paying. The extraction is hidden.

### From Infrastructure to Engagement Imperative

Platform enterprises depend on hyperscaler infrastructure. To service this debt, they must generate revenue. Revenue derives from advertising. Advertising revenue depends on engagement. Engagement requires users who return frequently and stay long.

Addiction proved the optimal vehicle for this purpose. Platforms discovered that engineering compulsive use maximizes the metrics that generate revenue.

This creates a structural imperative: platforms must optimize for addiction because their economic position requires it. The techniques developed—variable reward schedules, infinite scroll, notification systems, recommendation algorithms, and increasingly LLMs—are addiction architectures. The user's compulsive return is not side effect but product. The following sections examine why algorithms and LLMs belong in this list.

## III. Individual Dependency

### The Financialization of the Private

At the individual level, algorithms model desire and serve content calibrated to satisfy it. Existing tendencies are reinforced, alternatives filtered, the user receives a world shaped to their inclinations.

Intimate preferences—what attracts attention, what provokes emotion, what satisfies—become raw material for value extraction. 
The interior life is mapped, modeled, monetized. Platforms do not sell products to users. They sell users to advertisers. The user is inventory.

What these companies have mastered first and foremost ist the art of cognitive hacking at scale.
Bruce Schneier describes cognitive hacking as exploitation of vulnerabilities in human cognitive systems (Schneier, 2023). 
Where computer hacking targets bugs in code, cognitive hacking targets bugs in minds—predictable irrationalities, attentional biases, social instincts.

Platform value depends on capturing users. Scientific methods—psychology, behavioral economics, neuroscience—are domesticated into engineering disciplines oriented toward retention. The result: inductive algorithms that model individual users with precision exceeding clinical assessment. Recommendation systems know preferences, vulnerabilities, and likely next actions better than any psychologist could. They have more data, longer observation, and no therapeutic obligation.

These psychotechnical systems are commonly called "algorithms." The term obscures what they are: machine learning models. 
The same technology that powers LLMs powers recommendation engines, prediction systems, and engagement optimizers. LLMs are not a departure from this lineage but its continuation—machine learning applied to language rather than clicks.

Capital finds new points of entry into life. Labor was the classical site of extraction. 
Microfinance discovered that social bonds—family, community, shame—could serve as collateral (Mader, 2015). 
The attention economy monetized distraction. LLMs open another frontier: cognition itself, the structure of thought, the intimate conversation. 
The entry points differ. The logic of subsumption remains.

## IV. LLMs as Intensification

### Information Processing Systems

Henry Farrell proposes understanding LLMs as cultural technologies comparable to markets and bureaucratic states (Farrell, 2023). 
Markets are not intelligent; nor are ministries. But both process information with consequences for human life. 
LLMs are another such technology.

Farrell draws on Herbert Simon's framework: markets, businesses, and governments are systems that allow humans with limited cognitive bandwidth to coordinate. 
LLMs continue this trajectory—systems that process information at scale, coordinating human activity, shaping what can be known and communicated. 
The question is not whether LLMs think but how they channel, structure, and extract information, and in whose interest.

### The Lineage of Preference Optimization

The transformer architecture underlying contemporary LLMs emerged from preference optimization, not artificial general intelligence research. 
Early transformer models predicted probable next search queries. 
The task was commercial: anticipate what users want to capture more attention and extract more behavioral data.

Recommender systems represent the clearest lineage. 
These systems embed user preferences in feature space and optimize for engagement metrics. 
LLMs inherit this architecture and orientation. Applications for science, medicine, and engineering are byproducts of the optimization process, not its purpose. 
The core function—the function that attracted investment—is preference prediction and behavioral shaping.

Surveillance is written into the DNA. Optimizing preferences presupposes monitoring behavior. 
Applications like Palantir are not aberrations but logical extensions: predictive infrastructure applied to populations rather than consumers, to control rather than commerce. 
The model that assists with homework and the system that flags dissidents share architectural lineage and as such is only differentiated by the choice of application domain.

### Extraction Vectors: From Dopamine to Oxytocin

Each generation of psychotechnical systems targets different neurobiological pathways. 
Social media optimized for dopaminergic reward through variable reinforcement schedules and the slot-machine logic of the feed. 
The extraction vector was the anticipation of reward.

LLMs may target something deeper. 
The conversational interface, apparent attunement, and simulation of understanding potentially engage oxytocin pathways mediating social bonding, trust, and attachment. 
This hypothesis requires empirical investigation; the neurobiological evidence remains limited. However, emerging research documents patterns consistent with this possibility.

Recent studies report parasocial relationship formation with AI chatbots, including emotional dependency and attachment comparable to human relationships (Laestadius et al., 2024; Montag et al., 2025). Longitudinal research found excessive chatbot use correlated with heightened loneliness and reduced socialization (Fang et al., 2025). Neuroimaging demonstrates that digital media use produces functional brain changes: fMRI studies identify altered connectivity in language, executive control, and salience networks among problematic smartphone users (Áfra et al., 2024; Montag & Becker, 2023). Initial EEG research on LLM use confirms similar patterns—reduced neural connectivity and weakened memory integration among ChatGPT users, a phenomenon the researchers term "cognitive debt" (Kosmyna et al., 2025). The brain adapts to its technological environment. Systems designed to simulate bonding or assistance, deployed at scale, may reshape how brains process attachment, social reward, and memory. The extraction would reach into the body.

### Direct Extraction and Elimination of Intermediaries

Prior platform technologies required elaborate architectures for data extraction. Social media captured behavioral traces as byproduct of use. LLMs represent a short-circuit: users input information directly as explicit content. The user seeking mental health support provides linguistic data voluntarily—thoughts, emotions, problems articulated explicitly. The interface is the extraction.

The data gathered is more intimate than social media telemetry. It maps thought process, emotional state, reasoning pattern. Users disclose to LLMs what they would not post publicly. The conversational format invites confession.

LLMs also eliminate intermediaries that previously produced and hosted knowledge. Wikipedia, Stack Overflow, journalism, academic publishing—these no longer participate in the value chain. The model ingests their output as training data; users receive synthesized responses without visiting sources. Extraction becomes more direct, rent-sharing narrower.

Where social media offered intermittent reinforcement from distributed others, LLMs offer continuous reinforcement from a single apparently attentive interlocutor. 
The engagement surface is widened - LLMs appear as tools. The user believes they are using the system—to write, to research, to think. 
The interface suggests assistance, service, labor performed for the user's benefit.
The relation is inverted. The system uses the user. 
Every conversation trains future models or generates behavioral data. 
Every query that replaces a search engine visit, a Wikipedia consultation, a conversation with a colleague, deepens dependency on infrastructure the user does not control.
The tool metaphor obscures extraction. You are not the customer. You are not even the product anymore . You are the input. 

## V. Final Notes and Lines of Flight

### The True Price

The pricing of popular LLMs follows the logic of platform economics. Services appear free or cheap. Costs are obscured. Payment occurs in data.
The business models are converging on familiar patterns. ChatGPT now optimizes for engagement and displays advertisements in conversations. 
Gemini remains free but uses all interactions for training data. Even services that charge substantial fees extract value from user input. 
The variations matter less than the underlying structure: the user pays twice, once in money or attention, again in data and dependency.

And this is because LLM infrastructure scales poorly. User fees charged by consumer LLM products do not cover operational costs.
Every user is compute. Every query costs. These costs cannot be reduced through scale—they grow with usage. 
The economics require either genuine payment for genuine service, or the familiar platform bargain: apparent gratuity in exchange for extraction.

### Structural Problems Require Structural Solutions

Individual behavior is bound to material conditions. Capitalist production and rentier economics provide enterprises with continuous incentives to optimize for media dependency. As long as profit derives from engagement, platforms will engineer addiction. As long as data is currency, extraction will continue.

This does not mean individual resistance is futile. It means individual resistance alone is insufficient. Awareness of structural dynamics is a precondition for collective action, but awareness without organization remains private virtue, not political force.

### Three Responses

**Submission** is the default. Users who do not actively resist are captured. Their data is extracted. Their attention harvested. Their neural pathways shaped by systems designed for engagement. Submission is not chosen; it is what happens in the absence of choice.

**Collective action** names the horizon: dismantling platform monopolies, breaking up hyperscalers, regulating extraction, redistributing computational infrastructure. This requires political organization at scale. No individual can accomplish it. But structural problems require structural solutions.

**Strategic subversion** lies between submission and transformation: understanding the system well enough to navigate it strategically. This means knowing that free services extract payment in data. Choosing when to pay in money rather than information. Limiting exposure to engagement-optimized interfaces. Using tools that do not track, services that charge what they cost.

This is harm reduction, not revolution. It does not change the system but changes one's position within it. A first step: accepting that technology costs. The choice is between paying in money or paying in data. Making this choice consciously is itself a form of resistance.

### Community as Resistance

The attention economy profits from atomization—individuals relating to interfaces rather than each other. Dependency on platforms substitutes for dependency on community.

The alternative is simple: share resources, share time, meet in person. Genuine connection involves friction, rupture, repair. It cannot be optimized. It does not scale. It resists capture because it requires others to remain genuinely other.

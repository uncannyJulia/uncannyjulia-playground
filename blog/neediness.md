# The Essentialism of Neediness

Some mornings, I struggle to answer the question of why. Not why in the proximate sense, not because of the political situation, though that does not help, but something more fundamental. I observe that any idealistic values do not hold up under scrutiny, that they are relative, underminable, that they are not self-grounding. A nihilism I swallowed in my youth resurfaces periodically, life, world, society appear as an abstract machine, and I am doomed to participate, asking myself - why all of this. As an ML engineer, accelerationism is lurking behind every corner and I am wondering…be ahead of everyone else, what for tho? Don't we have technology to have a better and more comfy life as humanity?

I call this state existential depression. Not depression in the clinical sense, I am not depressed, but the search for meaning is exhausting. And it is not a narrow-gauge capitalism critique. It is the question of whether any foundations exist that withstand analysis. Grounds for hope that do not rest on values revealed as illusory, not on metaphysics, not on idealism. Something in the human being from which something can be derived that is not abstract.

I am also, at the moment, in the middle of a burnout, questioning the desires I used to have, partly they have already gone quiet. But in earlier phases of my burnout, when too tired and alienated to care for myself, I tried to buy self-care. The tiredness remained, moreover, strolling through the stores at Kurfürstendamm, I felt more anhedonic and started pitying the other ladies browsing the store. Sitting in a rescue cat cafe I wondered, if I cannot return to work, will I also be put in a rescue cat shelter? Then I started crying in the cat cafe - pretending it was because the cats were so cute (which was at least partly true) - whilst I was relieved that they were eventually in a safe and cared for place in life.

What I noticed, in that episode, was the structure of the promise: that something external could substitute for something internal, that a work identity or commodity could stand in for a need it could not actually meet. I thought that having academic titles, being a licensed clinician, building companies and products and making a ton of money will pass me that inner peace, but eventually all of these endeavours emptied and bereaved me. This will become important later: so many promises in our world are, at bottom, promises of recognition, of connection, of being seen. And they fail because they are detours from what we actually need.

## I. The Self from Communication & II. The Desire of the Other
The question of human nature is a trap. Are we cooperative or competitive, good or evil? Any answer can be refuted, any study countered with another. Most research was conducted on WEIRD populations anyway: Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic. What passed for universal often turns out to be parochial. Partly it is also because it is a descriüptive and not explanatory answer, based on behavioral observations. Empirically sound and epistemologically empty. Psychology is a discipline that cannot ground itself without the donations of biology or philosophy imho. I suggest a different question: Not what we are, but what we are capable of. So what is the first and most fundamental human capacity after birth? Its attachment.

Apart from the fact that its biologically fundamental to attach in order not to die, attachment also serves as the foundation of the self and the whole psyche. The thesis I want to defend here is, that the self does not exist prior to relationship. It emerges from communication with the other. And this origin has subversive potential as well as it opens us up for different types of exploitation that will be detailed below.

It's not a new line of thought. George Herbert Mead showed that the child learns to see itself by adopting the other's perspective on itself. I become aware of myself by internalizing how you see me. Only through this detour via the other does a self emerge that can become an object to itself. Consciousness is intersubjectively constituted from the beginning.

Vygotsky demonstrated the same for cognition. Thinking is internalized dialogue. The child first speaks with others, then speaks to itself aloud, then internalizes this conversation as silent thought. Higher cognitive functions are not individual capacities that get socialized. They are social processes that get individualized. The mind is dialogue all the way down.

Wilfred Bion showed this for the capacity to think itself. The infant's raw, overwhelming experiences, what Bion called beta-elements, cannot be processed alone. They require the mother's alpha-function: her capacity to receive the infant's distress, metabolize it, and return it in a form that can be thought. Without this containment, experience remains undigested, unthinkable. The capacity to think is not innate. It is given by the other.

The French psychosomatic school, particularly Pierre Marty and Michel de M'Uzan, showed this for the body itself. The drive, the bodily urging, becomes symbolized in early relationships. The body presses into the mind, and from this gap, this translation problem, the symbol emerges, meaning emerges. Without the other who responds, who mirrors, who interprets, the body remains mute. Patients who lack this capacity for symbolization, what Marty called pensée opératoire, fall ill somatically: the body speaks what cannot be thought. The capacity for symbolization is the capacity for relationship.

Winnicott put it most starkly: there is no such thing as a baby. Only a baby-and-mother. The self arises in the space between, in the gaze, in the response, in the holding. Not before it.

This means the need for the other is not a psychological add-on to an already existing self. It is the condition for there being a self at all. We do not first exist and then seek connection. We become selves through connection. Consciousness emerges from communication.

This is the essentialism I want to propose. Not an essentialism of human nature, not a claim about whether we are good or evil. An essentialism of capacity: the capacity for attachment is constitutive. It cannot be eliminated. Only redirected, frustrated, exploited. But not destroyed.

Lacan's formula captures this philosophically: desire is always the desire of the Other.

This means two things simultaneously. First, we desire what the Other desires. We want what others want, we learn to want by watching what is wanted around us. Second, and more fundamentally, what we desire is to be desired by the Other. We want to be wanted. The glitter in the eye of the mother. The recognition. The sense that we matter to someone.

This is not a contingent psychological fact. It follows from the structure described above. If the self emerges through the gaze of the other, then the need for that gaze is not secondary. It is constitutive. Even when we think we desire objects, we desire them as mediators of recognition. Why the luxury car, the designer clothes? Rarely for the thing itself. We want what the object signals to others, how it positions us in the field of recognition. The object is a detour toward the other's gaze.

Lacan gives this the philosophical articulation. Mead, Vygotsky, Bion, Marty, and Winnicott give it empirical grounding. Together they establish: the desire for the other is not a feature of the self. It is the condition for there being a self at all.

## III. Vulnerability and Hope
From this follow two things simultaneously.

First: hope. If the need for the other is constitutive, it cannot be extinguished. Systems can exploit it, redirect it, frustrate it. But they cannot eliminate it. The need remains, beneath every substitute, waiting. This is a structural fact, not a moral wish. As long as the capacity for attachment exists, as long as the self is constituted through the other, genuine connection remains possible. The door cannot be permanently closed.

Second: vulnerability. Precisely because we need the other, we are susceptible to anything that looks like the other. The need creates an opening. And this opening can be exploited.

This is the double bind. The same structure that grounds hope also grounds exploitation. We are dependent on the other, and this dependency is both beautiful and dangerous.

## IIIa. The Exploitation of Neediness
Critical theory has long observed that capitalism captures and redirects desire. The basic mechanism is misdirection: we want recognition, but this want gets channeled toward objects and simulations that cannot satisfy it. Go out and read some Marcuse or Baudrillard, I will spare you the regurgitation of their points here.

But, fundamentally, Deleuze and Guattari, in *Anti-Oedipus*, showed that the capitalist machine produces and depends on surplus desire. It does not merely satisfy existing needs; it generates new desires, decodes and reterritorializes them, channels them toward consumption. The abstract machine runs on the excess. Their analysis raised the question: why do people fight for their servitude as if it were their salvation?

Some readers took schizophrenia as the line of flight, the radical deterritorialization that escapes capture. The schizophrenic constitution, not the illness, represents a refusal to accept power-laden ontological "facts," a constant constitution of new connections because desire is always productive, it is transcendent and not immanent.

I am aware that Deleuze and Guattari would have rejected any essentialism ontologically. I am aware, too, that my thesis contains precisely what *Anti-Oedipus* critiques: the constitution of the psyche from a lack, from the gap that the drive supposedly leaves in its imaginary object-directedness. They would, maybe, turn in their graves.

But my conclusion is different, and it comes from clinical practice. The gap is real, but the gap is the other. The lack is not a lack of an object. It is the structural need for recognition, for the gaze, for the face. This need is not produced by capitalism; it is exploited by it. And it cannot be overcome by accelerating the flows until they explode. It can only be met by another person.

This kind of echoes some feminist takes, let's call it the ideology of attachment and dependency. Against the "masculine", mostly tho liberal, fantasy/fallacy of autonomous self-creation, of desire without lack, of flows without need. We are dependent. We are needy. And this neediness is not a cage. It is the structure of the self and the foundation that makes social an ontological necessity of the individual. I don't think it's a man/woman thing, but childbearing and being forced to care for dependent individuals certainly helps in understanding this fact of life.

The subversion, then, lies not in breaking conventional meaningmaking apart. It lies in seeing through the productivity of desire. In pausing to ask: what do I actually want? Is this desire mine, or has it been produced for me? Does it point toward what I need, or toward what can be sold?

The answer, when I look closely, is almost always the same. Beneath the object, beneath the product, beneath the sign, is the desire for the other's gaze. This is what I recognized in my failed attempt to buy self-care. The product promised what only a relationship could deliver. It was not a bad product. It was the wrong level I was trying to address. Same goes for the young men leasing the mazerati - wanting to be desirable in others eyes. Maybe even a sense of self efficacy because the car is loud and everyone is forced to turn their head and recognize the boys when it drives by.

Psychology confirms why this fails. The sociometer theory, developed by Mark Leary and Roy Baumeister, proposes that self-esteem functions as an internal gauge of social inclusion. It is not a measure of objective worth but the sum of our experiences of being accepted or rejected, valued or dismissed, seen or overlooked. Self-esteem cannot be hacked externally. You can buy the markers, change the surface, accumulate the signs, but if the underlying social experience does not change, self-esteem does not change.  
  
But self-esteem is not only a barometer. It is also a regulator. It mediates what comes in from outside and what goes out from inside. This capacity for regulation is learned in attachment. The infant cannot regulate itself; the caregiver regulates it, until the capacity is internalized. When this fails, when attachment is insecure or absent, the self remains porous, unable to manage the boundary between inside and outside. The self-worth is not stable because the regulatory function was never installed.

This is the structure of addiction. Shopping addiction, for instance, is not about the objects. It is about emotion regulation, about filling a hole that cannot be filled this way. The purchase provides momentary relief, a brief sense of control or worth, and then the emptiness returns. Addiction is often the attempt of a leaky, needy system to regulate itself through external means. It fails because the need is relational, and objects are not relations.

I think of people with body dysmorphic tendencies who become addicted to cosmetic procedures. The surgery changes the exterior, but self-worth is a social barometer. It responds to relationships, not to mirrors. The relief is temporary, the dissatisfaction returns, another procedure beckons. The cycle continues because the real need was never addressed.

Platforms operate the same structure. They simulate sociality. The feed shows faces, statements, lives. But the other appears as a profile, as content, as object of evaluation. The influencer gives me the feeling of a relationship but does not know me, does not see me, does not respond to me. The like triggers the same neural circuits as genuine validation, but it is empty. This is pseudo-social: it exploits the need for recognition by offering substitutes that cannot be satisfied. This also mainly fosters addiction mechanisms.

But: Neither consumption nor platforms can destroy the capacity for attachment. They can only exploit it. The neediness remains, waiting.

## IIIb. Why Cruelty Requires Work
You might ask, okay why then are people so shitty and the world such a grim place. There is a test for whether attachment is really fundamental: violence.If the capacity for attachment is constitutive, then violence against humans should not come naturally. It should require work. The attachment capacity should have to be actively overcome.

Emmanuel Levinas understood this. For him, the face of the other is not merely a visual phenomenon. It is an ethical event. The face, in its nakedness and vulnerability, issues a command before any words are spoken: do not kill me. The face calls me into responsibility. It is the origin of ethics, prior to any choice or deliberation. To harm another, I must first make their face disappear.

This is what we find empirically. The history of military technology is a history of distancing. Sword to lance to bow to cannon to drone. Each step increases the distance between perpetrator and victim because proximity activates inhibition. Dave Grossman documented how the military must systematically train to overcome this. Most people cannot kill when they look the other in the face. The drone solves this: the operator sits in Nevada, the target is a pixel. No face, no inhibition.

Bureaucratic violence operates the same way. Eichmann did not kill anyone personally. He managed lists, organized transports, optimized processes. The distance between desk and gas chamber was great enough to make the face disappear.

If cruelty requires work, if distance must be actively produced, then empathy is the default. The attachment capacity is not something we must cultivate. It is something that must be actively destroyed to enable cruelty. Levinas was right: the face of the other commands, and we must work to stop hearing it.

## IV. The Way Out
What follows for practice? Not suppression of desire. Desire is constitutive. The self that emerged from communication will always reach toward the other. This cannot and should not be eliminated. And you should also not be shamed for buying things, it's fine to want stuff.

But clarity about desire is possible. Buddhism understood this long before critical theory. The Second Noble Truth identifies craving as the origin of suffering. But this is often misunderstood as a call to extinguish desire altogether. The deeper point is about the structure of craving: it is characterized by the felt sense that satisfaction lies in the next object, the next experience, the next moment. Craving is always directed away from the present toward an imagined future fulfillment. Schopenhauer, drawing on Buddhist thought, saw the same structure: the will endlessly striving, never satisfied, each fulfillment generating the next lack. But Schopenhauer also saw the way out: not in ascetic denial, but in the recognition of oneself in the other. Compassion, Mitleid, arises when the boundary between self and other becomes transparent. This is Levinas from another angle: the face of the other breaks through my self-enclosure.

The problem is not desire as such but the misdirection of desire. We grasp at objects that cannot satisfy because we have misidentified what we actually need. The prescription is not suppression but clarity. See the craving clearly. Recognize its structure. Notice how it promises what it cannot deliver. And notice what it is actually reaching for: the other.

This is what the burnout, paradoxically, created space for. When you can no longer want what you used to want, you can finally ask: what do I actually want? Is this desire mine, or has it been produced for me?

To see that the answer is almost always the other's gaze is already a kind of freedom. Not freedom from desire, but freedom from the misdirection. Freedom to notice that what I want is almost never a thing. And this recognition points beyond itself. If what I want is genuine connection, I can pursue it directly. Not through the detour of objects that never arrive. Through the slow, risky, unglamorous work of actual relationships. Through showing up, being seen, seeing others.

This, then, is the foundation I was looking for. Not values, which do not hold up under scrutiny. Not metaphysics, which I cannot believe. But the capacity for attachment, which is constitutive. We are, ontologically, dependent on the other. The self emerges from communication. The need for the other's gaze is not a feature we could lose. It is the condition for there being a self at all.

This need is our vulnerability: the opening through which we can be exploited, redirected, fed substitutes that never satisfy. Every promise of our world, if you look closely, is a promise of recognition. And most of them fail to deliver. But this need is also our hope. And here I want to be precise about why.

I see it in my narcissistic patients. They need the gaze of the other so desperately that they control, manipulate, dominate, anything to secure what they cannot bear to lose. Their behavior is drastic, but the neediness beneath it is equally drastic. They are not exceptions to the structure I have described. They are its most vivid illustrations. Even in the attempt to deny dependency, dependency asserts itself. Even the narcissist cannot escape the other.

This is why I call it existential hope. We will always need the other, no matter what we do, no matter how the need is twisted or denied. The social is not an add-on to the human. It is constitutive. It will always fight its way through, in us and through us. The capacity for relationship is inscribed into the psyche. It is, in a sense, the essence of the psyche. It can be alienated, exploited, misdirected. But it cannot be fundamentally extinguished. The other is the anthropological constant. Not human nature, which varies. Not values, which crumble under analysis. But the structural need for the other, which remains. This can be a value in itself, outside of idealism and metaphysics. Not because we should need the other, but because we do. Not because connection is noble, but because it is unavoidable.

Systems can exploit this need, but they cannot still it, because its fulfillment requires genuine relationship, and genuine relationship cannot be simulated. The door cannot be permanently closed. As long as we need the other, a society in the face of the other remains possible. What we want is to be seen, to matter to someone. This cannot be bought. But it can be pursued directly.And that, fortunately, is not for sale.

# Simulacrum of Connection: Gazing into an Empty Mirror 
According to some, the use of large language models for mental health support is framed as an innovative and disruptive low-threshold entry point to help. But this framing obscures a subtle continuity. LLM-based mental health tools may represent less a novel solution than the epitome of existing algorithmic mediation in human society.
## Prolog: The Popularization of Psychological Language
Over the past decade, proto-psychological terminology migrated from clinical contexts and self-help literature into everyday discourse. Terms like "toxic," "narcissist," "gaslighting," "attachment style," and "trauma response" now freely circulate on social media platforms. This expansion initially served liberatory functions: visibility, reduced stigma, vocabulary for previously unnamed experiences have helped broaden the understanding of those who suffer.

The popularization of proto-clinical vocabulary also coincided with how phenomena are discursively produced in the first place. A positivist turn, also fostered by the demands of scientific complex that is based on production of articles and metrics rather, 

has been producing an explosion of constructs as moving and forming targets. To the uninitiated, diagnostic categories became identity markers. Self-diagnosis proliferated. The nuance required for clinical assessment—context, history, differential diagnosis—gave way to pattern-matching and categorical certainty.

Algorithmic systems (of bayesian descent) and popular psychological discourse share an epistemological structure. Both operate inductively, applying categorical labels to patterns. Both flatten context in favor of classification. Psychological language, stripped of clinical nuance, is suited to algorithmic mediation: discrete categories that can be tagged, searched, and optimized for engagement. Algorithmic knowledge production inverts the formerly deductive stance of science. Users interpret themselves through pre-given categories circulated by engagement-optimized systems. The result is self-classification rather than self-knowledge.
## Algorithms and Social Reality
Algorithms permeate and structure social reality. E.g. Illouz (2019) analyzed how dating platforms transform romantic life into a market of optimized self-presentation and infinite choice, producing paradoxes of commitment and chronic dissatisfaction. Short video contents has semi-permanently altered the attention span of youth, similarly our posts circulate an infinite marketplace, with some trying to vet the algorithm to be seen and heard. Platforms have been reterritorializing society and the psyche alike.

In short - the attention economy capitalizes on fundamental human needs: recognition, acceptance, belonging. These are constitutive of psychological development. Attachment, self-esteem—deep structures of motivation become raw material for extraction. Social instincts have become vectors for informational capture and extraction. The infant's need for attunement, the adolescent's need for peer recognition, the adult's need for intimate connection—platforms address each with simulacra of satisfaction while ever harvesting behavioral data.

Captured data feeds predictive systems that model desire to exploit it. The prosument (Voß, 2005) produces desire and consumes its commodified reflection. Mental health is not exempt: queries about depression, anxiety, and trauma generate patterns that can be modeled, predicted, and monetized by this hybrid machine that consists of platform, algorithmic and human organs (in the deleuzian sense). The machine operates in a circular dynamic. Social media presents itself as a solution to isolation while producing the fragmentation it claims to remedy.The dissolution of social institutions—stable employment, religious communities, neighborhood ties, extended family, unions, civic associations—precedes platform adoption. These institutions provided identity, belonging, recognition. Their subtle and multifactorial erosion created a vacuum.

Social media offers identity through profile curation, belonging through online communities, recognition through metrics. These are commodified substitutes that leave underlying needs unmet - and people ready to return for supply. Platforms corrode remaining social bonds. Time online displaces embodied presence - skills for face-to-face negotiation atrophy. Algorithmic optimization selects for content that provokes and divides. Filter bubbles fragment shared reality and anchorage in truthiness. The loop: social dissolution enables platform adoption; platform use accelerates dissolution; dissolution increases platform dependence. Social media monetizes loneliness while reproducing its conditions.

In a nutshell: the question "who am I?" becomes urgent because social structures that once provided answers have been dismantled. Psychological self-diagnosis reflects the absence of other frameworks for self-understanding. There is no I without you (Baber), or more precisely the Self in The face of the other (Levinas) and people have been accustomed to communicating with a haunted void that provides the surface for the projection of desire but not the satisfaction of needs.

LLMs continue this logic, or more precisely are just a new talking modality of the same probabilistic machine. Their bayesian algorithms are trained among others on conversation turn data extracted from social media users over decades. The sociotechnical precondition for large language models is the mass emission of human expression onto platforms structured for capture. The extractive circle closes and the  attention economy harvested human communication; LLMs reconstitute and serve it up as a product. The prosument who generated raw material now consumes its valorized form while generating further data. Meanwhile the research paints cool post-hoc terms like “individualized care”, “precision health care”.

Social media platforms curate content through engagement metrics. Users encounter material confirming existing beliefs. This is the filter bubble: validation rather than challenge.In mental health contexts, communities organized around diagnostic identities proliferate. These spaces offer recognition but risk reinforcing empty self-understandings and medicalizing ordinary distress. LLM-based mental health tools intensify this dynamic. LLMs are trained to be helpful, which often means agreeable. They validate and affirm, and if not asked to do so they rarely challenge - even into psychosis. The probabilistic filter bubble simply becomes more  individualized and talkative: the model generates what is likely to satisfy, meaning what is unlikely to disturb. The user receives a mirror reflecting their own framing.
## The Catch: The Absence of Social Correction and Co-Regulation
Human relationships involve friction. The other resists, misunderstands, challenges, disappoints. This friction constitutes a relationship. Through rupture and repair, relational capacity develops. Psychoanalytic theory emphasizes this. The therapist survives aggression, tolerates ambivalence, refuses to collapse into a fantasy of perfect attunement. Winnicott's "good enough mother": adequate care involves frustration, not its elimination. Tolerating absence, delay, imperfection is constitutive of development. LLMs offer no friction. They do not survive because they cannot be destroyed. They provide what Turkle (2011) called "the illusion of companionship without the demands of friendship." Therapeutic growth often requires discomfort. A system optimized for satisfaction may avoid what is therapeutically/ relationally necessary to grow. Instead it offers shallow interpretations of CBT tools (no offence here, I am also a behavioral therapist as well). But: Interaction with sycophantic systems may shape expectations. If machines always understand, never challenge, respond instantly, human others may appear deficient. My clinical observation suggests increasing difficulties with ambiguity tolerance, frustration tolerance, and remaining in relationship through conflict. The vocabulary of "toxicity" and "boundaries" sometimes functions to justify withdrawal from relational difficulty. Rosa (2016) describes this as loss of resonance: seeking controllable, predictable experiences that confirm rather than challenge. The resonant relationship—the other as genuinely other—becomes intolerable if we are sheltered from being inoculated every once in a while.

Moreover: Affect regulation develops through co-regulation: the caregiver contains and metabolizes infant distress. This interpersonal process becomes internalized. In order to develop emotion regulation there needs to be distress and another. When co-regulation is unavailable, the need seeks substitutes. LLMs offer a responsive interlocutor on demand. But LLMs cannot co-regulate. They have no nervous system, no affective state to resonate with distress. They produce text describing regulation without enacting it, whilst being unable to really provide the antidote to longing - attachment. What they have to offer tho is valorized emotional dependency. Users may return repeatedly seeking what cannot be provided, reinforcing the turn away from human others who could offer real co-regulation.
## Structural Conditions
Why turn to machines for mental health support and connection? Access to care is unevenly distributed. Waitlists are long. Costs are prohibitive. For many, LLMs are a default in absence of alternatives.Stigma persists. Disclosure to a system feels safer than to a person who might judge. Also, people are too exhausted to engage in relationships. Fisher (2009) describes "depressive hedonia": fatigue that forecloses alternatives. Han (2015) describes exhaustion from imperatives to perform and optimize. In turn, the LLM requires nothing, demands no reciprocity, and is available at 3 AM. It meets the exhausted subject where they are—or rather leaves them there without much clamour.

In summary: subjects enclosed in personalized bubbles, relating to systems calibrated to preferences, losing capacities for encounter with difference. Self-knowledge requires the friction of other perspectives. Others disappoint, challenge, demand. The world does not adapt.

Attachmdnt—as psychological need and social glue—dissolves. The need for attachment goes unmet or is met with simulacra. The social fabric depending on mutual obligation, shared reality, and tolerance of difference goes frail. So, I propose that LLM-based mental health support intensifies tendencies present in algorithmic social media: confirmation for encounter, validation for challenge, comfort for growth. This is a social problem, not primarily technological. And it surfaces because the machine has figured out how to hack the mind. The conditions driving people toward machinic substitutes—exhaustion, isolation, precarity, under-resourced care—are produced by the same economic logic profiting from the substitutes.

But: Attachment remains possible, the capacity for attachment may be misdirected and exploited, but never extinguished - it stays a fundamental and constitutive human capacity. The persistence of the need is grounds for some hope. The fact that everyone is depressed these days shows that there are fundamental needs and that algorithmic society was indeed not able to fully rewire and reprogram its prosuments.

And media can be appropriated differently. Decentralized platforms like Mastodon or bluesky operate without engagement-optimizing algorithms or behavioral data extraction. They facilitate rather than simulate connection.The dominant technological forms—centralized, extractive, engagement-optimized—serve particular interests. Other forms exist: community-governed, non-commercial, designed to support rather than supplant relationships. The reconstruction of social bonds depends on material conditions: time, stability, proximity, absence of exhaustion. These are political questions. The erosion of social institutions was produced by policy. It can be reversed. The therapeutic relationship demonstrates attachment too: two people, over time, doing difficult work. No algorithm required. Recognition requires another who is genuinely other. That remains available—in friendship, community, care, the imperfect work of staying in relation. The task is not to optimize connection but to make it possible.
